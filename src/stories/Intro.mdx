## Objective and approach

Using the term *design system* is both helpful and misleading.
Building a design system requires the context of a product or organisation, which this project lacks.
What it resembles more closely is a *design repertoire* — a personal collection of patterns that shape how I approach design problems↔solutions.
The project uses design practice itself as the material being organised and explored.

The repertoire is shaped by a context of [my work](https://igorrazvodovsky.com/): professional work-support systems — tools that facilitate work, coordination, and information-intensive activities.
This is worth stating because it determines what patterns appear here, how they get evaluated, and which use [qualities](..?path=/docs/qualities-overview--docs) receive attention.


In practical terms the objective is to map relationships between patterns and understand how they connect,
while approaching the process like tending a garden — cultivating ideas over time, refining structures and fostering connections.
I'm interested in two layers: patterns as components (the structural building blocks) and patterns as carriers of experiential qualities — malleability, agency, accessibility, transparency, and other dimensions of what it feels like to use something.

## On LLMs and AI

What started as an implementation-agnostic exploration of interaction patterns pivoted twice.
In 2025 it became a playground for weaving LLMs into component-based structures, using design systems as a lens to explore what was emerging.
By 2026 the focus shifted toward [generative UI](https://github.com/igorrazvodovsky/gen-ui-playground) specification — exploring how this repertoire might serve as a knowledge base for task-grounded generation.

## Now

A sporadically updated log of current focus.

### April '26

Agency keeps splitting. [Assistance](../?path=/docs/foundations-assistance--docs) and [delegation](../?path=/docs/foundations-delegation--docs) graduated into their own foundations, and *appropriate reliance* was added to agency itself.

The pattern graph picked up typed edges and a labels layer (renamed from *gloss*), so connections now carry meaning rather than just existing, which in turn opens up filtering and rendering moves that flat links couldn't support.


### March

Reading Bowker and Star's [Sorting Things Out](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262522953/sorting-things-out/) and Alexander's [A City Is Not a Tree](https://www.patternlanguage.com/archive/cityisnotatree.html) prompted a rethink of how the project is organised. Two moves followed: reorganising the main nav around [activity theory](https://ixdf.org/literature/book/the-encyclopedia-of-human-computer-interaction-2nd-ed/activity-theory) levels to make the structure more generative; and building a graph where clusters emerge from link structure rather than category assignments, opening up other ways to navigate the project.

Running alongside: [genUI playground](https://github.com/igorrazvodovsky/gen-ui-playground), where this repertoire feeds into generative UI experiments.

### January

I've been reading [Thoughtful Interaction Design](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262622097/thoughtful-interaction-design/) and it's shifting how I think about this project.
Löwgren & Stolterman's design repertoire — a personal collection that structures how a designer sees new situations — maps loosely onto this project. But their repertoire is tacit and embodied; this one is external and explicit. That gap raises a question: how does it actually feed back into practice? An LLM-powered workflow might be the answer.

Alongside this, I'm looking for a more sustainable way to approach what Löwgren & Stolterman call use-oriented qualities — experiential dimensions like malleability, tight coupling, transparency, fluency.
Throughout the year, the *foundations* list kept growing, and mixing qualities from different rungs of the abstraction ladder—practical versus purely visual—made it feel incoherent.

### November '25

*Collaboration* and *conversation* have become central this autumn.
I've added [conversation](../?path=/docs/qualities-conversation--docs) as a foundational layer—treating all interaction as coordinated turn-taking.

This led to expanding the [collaboration](../?path=/docs/activities-collaboration--docs) patterns, particularly around human↔bot dynamics.
The collaboration stages now map how [agency patterns](../?path=/docs/qualities-agency--docs) shift throughout co-creation. I've been particularly interested in how humans elaborate on AI contributions—from surface acceptance to integrative advancement—and what that means for maintaining agency whilst working with generative systems.

### August

This summer I've been using [concept design](http://essenceofsoftware.com/) to tackle LLMs — breaking
AI down into user-facing semantic blocks rather than mapping technology directly to interaction.
This approach feels more promising than my previous attempts, but I haven't built anything with these concepts yet.
It's possible I'm overextending Jackson's methodology, so I might scale back the LLM concept set.

On the practical side, I'm vibing my way through block-based text patterns enabling [collaboration](../?path=/docs/activities-collaboration--docs):
commenting, [referencing](../?path=/docs/actions-coordination-bubble-menu--docs), highlighting, etc. Thinking about collaboration led to updating foundations —
I've added [conversation](../?path=/docs/qualities-conversation--docs) and [formality](../?path=/docs/qualities-formality--docs).
Conversation now exists at three levels (foundation, pattern, and concept), which either reflects its central role in interaction design or signals I'm overcomplicating things.
Formality, on the other hand, remains purely inspirational for now—no patterns implementing it yet.

### May

I continue building the map of LLM interaction patterns. Right now exploring how user intent gets captured and acted on. There are three interconnected tracks:
1. First, I've pulled apart [chat](../?path=/docs/actions-coordination-messaging--docs) and [bot](../?path=/docs/activities-bot--docs)—chat is where the [conversation](../?path=/docs/activities-conversation--docs) happen; the bot is the part that gets things done, often outside the chat. Seeing them separately makes it easier to think about assistants that shift between interface roles: from conversation partner to background actor.
2. This separation reveals that the chatbot implements a cluster of patterns revolving around clear, deliberate user intent provided via [prompt](../?path=/docs/activities-prompt--docs), and supported by mechanics like prompt guidance and scaffolding, and model switching.
3. This distinction, in turn, points toward another set of patterns—ways to handle unclear intent when the user doesn't or can't say what they want.
