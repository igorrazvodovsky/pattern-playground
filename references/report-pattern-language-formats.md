# Generative Pattern Language Formats

## Executive synthesis

The field has split into two strong but incomplete camps: **human-readable narrative frameworks** that preserve context, problem, forces, and design rationale, and **machine-readable execution layers** that formalize components, flows, variants, and implementation constraints. The most genuinely generative work now sits where those camps are fused: **an ontology- or knowledge-graph-backed pattern system with typed relations, queryable postconditions, and a workflow or executable layer for moving from one pattern to the next**. In other words, the strongest answer today is a **hybrid format**, not a pure narrative template, not a pure JSON schema, and not a pure ontology. On the evidence, the approaches that come closest to ŌĆ£strongŌĆØ generativity are: **graph-native pattern knowledge bases** that model patterns as nodes with typed edges and machine-checkable constraints; **model-driven UI specifications** such as IFML that make interaction flow, state transitions, and variation explicit enough to generate working variants; and **ontology-template systems** such as OPL/OTTR that encode reusable semantic building blocks, dependencies, and composition logic. By contrast, most mainstream UX and design-system practice still formalizes **components, tokens, and props**, not full pattern languages; those formats are extremely useful operationally, but they are too low-level to carry the full problemŌĆōforcesŌĆōconsequences logic that makes a language generative. A clear judgment follows from that split: **if the goal is a modern pattern language for UX/product/interaction design with possible LLM support, the best format today is a layered hybrid**: human-readable entries for authors and practitioners; a typed graph/ontology layer for relations, resulting contexts, and reasoning; and optional executable bindings to UI/component and flow specifications for implementation. Without all three layers, pattern languages tend to become either richly descriptive but inert, or executable but semantically thin. ## Comparative landscape

The **narrative-framework** tradition remains very much alive, but its most credible contemporary forms are no longer just ŌĆ£pattern cards.ŌĆØ A good example is the CHI 2024 framework of **six design principles for generative AI applications**, where each principle is tied to actionable strategies and validated against real-world products and internal product-team use. That is much stronger than a generic style guide, but relation semantics between principles remain mostly conceptual rather than computational, and sequencing is still largely left to human judgment. Older XML-first pattern efforts such as **PLML** are still visible in HCI pattern communities and even included a `resulting-context` element, but they are better understood today as historical baselines than as the center of current practice. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn9view7Ņłéturn35view1Ņłéturn35view2Ņłéturn27view0Ņłéturn39search0Ņłéturn39search13Ņłü

The **design-system metadata stack** is where most machine-readability in day-to-day UX practice now lives. The W3C Design Tokens format standardizes reusable decisions with **groups, aliases, `$extends`, property-level references, composite tokens, and deprecation fields**. Custom Elements Manifest captures component APIs in JSON so tools can auto-generate docs, demos, wrappers, warnings, and compatibility layers. StorybookŌĆÖs `args` and `argTypes` let teams generate interactive controls and reusable stories from component metadata. WICGŌĆÖs Open UI research process goes further by representing design systems in JSON and explicitly analyzing **anatomy, states, behaviors, and concepts** across real-world systems. This stack is practical, current, and highly interoperable, but it usually describes **implementation primitives**, not the higher-order logic of when one design pattern should follow, precondition, or override another. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn9view2Ņłéturn14view1Ņłéturn14view2Ņłéturn14view3Ņłéturn14view5Ņłéturn13view2Ņłéturn13view1Ņłéturn13view0Ņłéturn34view1Ņłéturn34view2Ņłü

The **model-based executable** camp is strongest where the artifact to be generated is an interface or interaction flow. IFML is still the most mature formal language in this space: it has machine-readable metamodels and mappings to platform-specific models, and it is explicitly for expressing content, user interaction, and control behavior of software front ends. Recent work extends this with **delta modeling** for UI variants, allowing add/remove/modify operations on IFML elements and automated generation of customizable UI variants from feature models. Two recent automation lines make the approach more contemporary: **DemGen** transforms design documents into formal IFML GUI models, and ICWE 2025 work uses LLMs to derive IFML-like GUI models from mock-ups, then passes them to deterministic code generators. This is real generativity, but it is strongest for navigable UI structure and variability, not for broader design rationale or service-level pattern composition. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn3search3Ņłéturn17view2Ņłéturn17view0Ņłéturn18view0Ņłéturn19search7Ņłü

The **ontology and modular-pattern** camp is where relation semantics are most explicit. Contemporary Ontology Pattern Language work describes an OPL as a **network of interrelated domain-related ontology patterns plus a process** that guides what problems arise, what order to address them in, and which patterns to apply; it explicitly names relations such as **dependency, composition, and variant** and treats sequencing as part of the language, not optional commentary. OTTR advances this toward execution: its 2024 framework formalizes templating, serialization, template libraries, bulk instantiation from tabular or queryable sources, and industrial use cases. OPLa adds metadata and annotations that support tooling, and its pattern-based KG visualization work shows how pattern annotations can guide exploration through **meaningful thematic paths**. These approaches are semantically strong and machine-actionable, but they are still heavier than most design teams will tolerate unless insulated by better authoring tools. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn11view0Ņłéturn26search15Ņłéturn11view1Ņłéturn37view0Ņłéturn37view1Ņłéturn11view2Ņłéturn38search0Ņłéturn38search12Ņłéturn38search6Ņłéturn38search8Ņłü

The most advanced **graph-native hybrid** work combines explicit semantics with recommendation and interactive access. The 2024 CHI **dark-pattern ontology** harmonizes ten academic and regulatory taxonomies into a three-level ontology and explicitly frames how low-level, meso-level, and high-level patterns can connect to detection, harms, regulation, and case law. EuroUSEC 2024 then shows what operationalization looks like: it maps types to attributes, defines required/optional/non-required characteristics, and proposes **observable and measurable visual, process, and language features** so detection becomes less subjective. In adjacent computational design, the 2025 **KG-PLUB** system is especially important because it moves all the way into a graph-backed, LLM-supported recommendation loop: it uses OWL/RDF, SHACL validation, ontology-guided extraction, Neo4j/Cypher, workshop testing, and expert evaluation to recommend biodiversity design solutions and show measurable learning gains. More broadly, recent reviews of KGŌĆōLLM integration conclude that hybrid systems improve factual grounding, interpretability, and knowledge-intensive decision support compared with text-only LLM use. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn21view0Ņłéturn24view0Ņłéturn32view3Ņłéturn32view2Ņłéturn33view0Ņłéturn33view1Ņłéturn33view2Ņłéturn36view0Ņłéturn36view2Ņłéturn9view8Ņłü

Taken together, the landscape is clear. **Narrative schemas** are best for comprehension and workshop use. **Design-system schemas** are best for implementation interoperability. **Model-based specs** are best for executable UI generation. **Ontology/pattern networks** are best for explicit relation semantics and reasoning. **Knowledge-graph hybrids** are currently the strongest route to a genuinely generative pattern language because they can connect narrative, typed relations, validation, querying, and AI-assisted recommendation in one system. The field is therefore fragmented, but it is fragmented in a way that points to a coherent hybrid architecture rather than to indecision. ## Strongest current candidates

**Graph-native ontology-backed pattern knowledge bases** are the most promising contemporary approach for a truly generative language. They allow patterns to be modeled as nodes, relation types as edges, attributes as properties, and constraints as machine-checkable shapes; they also support recommendation, traversal, and LLM-assisted querying. KG-PLUB is not a general UX standard, but conceptually it is the clearest demonstration of what a modern generative pattern system can do: formalize design knowledge, validate it, and use it interactively in decision support with empirical evaluation. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn32view1Ņłéturn32view2Ņłéturn32view3Ņłéturn33view0Ņłéturn33view1Ņłéturn33view2Ņłü

**IFML-based model-driven UI specifications** matter because they already make interaction logic, events, containers, actions, and generated variants explicit enough to drive software output. The 2024 delta-modeling work and the 2025 mock-up-to-IFML pipeline show that this tradition is not dormant; it is evolving by adding variability management and LLM-based model extraction while keeping deterministic generation in the loop. If the target is a UI-heavy pattern language that must generate actual screen flows, this is one of the strongest available options. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn17view2Ņłéturn17view0Ņłéturn18view0Ņłéturn19search7Ņłü

**OPL plus OTTR** is the strongest current formal approach to typed relationships and reusable composition. OPL contributes the language of dependency, composition, variants, and process flow; OTTR contributes template instantiation, serialization formats, template libraries, integrity checks, and industrial deployment patterns. This stack is especially important if the pattern language is expected to support reuse, reasoning, multi-author maintenance, or alignment with formal knowledge-engineering workflows. **The design-system metadata stack**ŌĆöWICG Open UI research artifacts, Design Tokens, Custom Elements Manifest, Storybook, and related schemasŌĆömatters because it is where mainstream teams already maintain machine-readable design knowledge. It captures anatomy, states, behaviors, props, references, variants, and implementation metadata well enough to automate docs, controls, wrappers, and some testing and rendering. It is not a full pattern language, but it is the most realistic ŌĆ£operational layerŌĆØ for bringing a higher-order pattern language into design and engineering practice. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn13view0Ņłéturn34view1Ņłéturn34view2Ņłéturn9view2Ņłéturn14view1Ņłéturn14view2Ņłéturn13view2Ņłéturn13view1Ņłü

**Validated narrative principles-and-strategies frameworks** are still among the strongest options for the authoring and teaching layer. The CHI 2024 generative-AI principles show how to retain readability while increasing practical force: a small number of principles, nested implementation strategies, iterative refinement, and validation against commercial products and product teams. Their weakness is precisely what makes them insufficient on their own: they are strong on guidance, weak on formal relation semantics, queryability, and execution. The most important practical conclusion is that the **top candidates are complementary, not mutually exclusive**. Graph/ontology systems are best for the language backbone; IFML-like models are best for executable interaction flow; design-system schemas are best for implementation linkage; and narrative principles remain best for human comprehension and adoption. ## Implications for practice

For a team building a contemporary pattern language today, the main mistake would be to choose **only one** of the existing families. A pure narrative repository will remain interpretable but mostly descriptive. A pure component/schema stack will be operational but will collapse higher-order design knowledge into props and tokens. A pure ontology will be semantically elegant but too difficult to author and maintain without a human-friendly layer. The practical goal should therefore be to separate concerns: let authors work in a readable pattern form, let machines consume a typed graph, and let implementation systems attach executable fragments where appropriate. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35view0Ņłéturn13view0Ņłéturn9view2Ņłéturn11view1Ņłéturn32view3Ņłü

A modern pattern format should explicitly include what many current systems still underspecify: **typed relations, applicability conditions, and resulting context**. OPLŌĆÖs dependency/composition/variant logic shows why relation semantics cannot be left as generic ŌĆ£related patterns.ŌĆØ IFML shows why sequencing has to be represented as actual interaction flow, not just prose. The dark-pattern ontology and its EuroUSEC operationalization show why patterns also need a path from abstract type to observable criteria, otherwise validation stays subjective. And KG-PLUB shows that once those structures are explicit, recommendation and guided traversal become possible. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn11view0Ņłéturn17view0Ņłéturn24view0Ņłéturn33view1Ņłü

Just as important is what **should not** be treated as sufficient. Design Tokens, Storybook, CEM, and Open UI research artifacts are valuable, but they should be treated as **attachments or implementation bindings**, not as the pattern language itself. They are about realizations of patternsŌĆöcomponent anatomy, props, references, variants, states, and rendered behaviorŌĆönot about the upstream design problem, forces, downstream consequences, or why a particular pattern should be selected next. Teams that confuse these levels usually end up with a very good design system and a very poor pattern language. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn9view2Ņłéturn13view1Ņłéturn13view2Ņłéturn34view3Ņłü

Evidence handling is also underdeveloped across the field. Current systems do show promising fragments: provenance across multiple taxonomies, measurable features for reproducibility, expert panels, workshop data, statistical testing, deprecation markers, integrity checks, and template-library maintenance. But there is still no widely adopted, cross-domain standard for **confidence scores, evidence strength, or applicability confidence** at the pattern-entry level. That means any serious modern pattern language should add these fields explicitly rather than waiting for the field to converge. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn21view0Ņłéturn24view0Ņłéturn33view0Ņłéturn33view1Ņłéturn14view1Ņłéturn37view1Ņłü

One more implication is disciplinary: among the strongest 2020+ examples, **computational design, ontology engineering, and model-driven UI are ahead of mainstream UX and service design** in machine-actionable pattern formats. That is not because UX lacks patterns; it is because UX practice has standardized lower in the stackŌĆöcomponents, tokens, and docsŌĆöwhile graph-native and ontology-backed pattern languages remain more common in adjacent computational fields. For UX/product teams, the opportunity is not to invent from scratch, but to import the best ideas from those adjacent fields without inheriting their authoring burden wholesale. ## Recommendation

The best-practice recommendation today is a **hybrid pattern-language format with three synchronized layers**: **MDX or Markdown with structured front matter for human authoring**, **JSON-LD or RDF/OWL backed by SHACL for the canonical semantic model**, and **a property-graph deployment view** for traversal, recommendation, and LLM-assisted retrieval. Where implementation linkage matters, add references to **design-system artifacts** such as Design Tokens, CEM, Storybook stories, or UI-flow models such as IFML. This recommendation follows directly from the strengths and weaknesses of the current landscape: narrative frameworks remain best for readability, graph/ontology systems remain best for relations and reasoning, and design-system/model-based formats remain best for execution. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35view0Ņłéturn32view3Ņłéturn11view1Ņłéturn17view0Ņłéturn13view2Ņłéturn13view1Ņłéturn9view2Ņłü

**Required fields** should be explicit and minimal, not encyclopedic: stable `id`; `name`; `summary`; `pattern_type`; `context`; `problem`; `forces`; `solution`; `applicability`; `contraindications`; `resulting_context`; `consequences`; `examples`; `evidence`; `confidence`; `status`; `version`; `provenance`; and `implementation_links`. If a team cannot fill in `applicability` and `resulting_context` in a structured way, the entry is probably descriptive guidance, not a generative pattern. This recommendation is consistent with the gaps visible in current component schemas and the stronger semantics visible in ontology and graph systems. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn13view0Ņłéturn9view2Ņłéturn11view0Ņłéturn32view3Ņłü

**Required relation types** should be typed, directional, and intentionally small in number: `prerequisite_of` / `depends_on`; `next_after` / `precedes`; `part_of` / `composed_of`; `specializes` / `generalizes`; `alternative_to`; `compatible_with`; `conflicts_with`; `implements_via`; and `evidenced_by`. These cover the strongest relation families that current research and tooling actually rely on: dependency, composition, alternatives, sequencing, specialization, realization, and validation. Anything weaker than thisŌĆöespecially a generic `related_to` edgeŌĆöwill not support coherent generation or recommendation. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn11view0Ņłéturn17view0Ņłéturn24view0Ņłü

**Resulting context** should not be a loose prose note. Represent it in two forms at once: a short human-readable paragraph and a machine-readable object containing **postconditions** such as enabled capabilities, changed user/system state, new constraints, likely follow-on problems, and recommended next-pattern candidates. This is the practical modern equivalent of the older ŌĆ£resulting contextŌĆØ idea from HCI pattern markup, but it must now be queryable and traversable if the language is meant to generate coherent sequences. **Sequencing and composition** should be represented as a lightweight workflow layer over the graph. The graph stores typed edges; the workflow layer stores conditional traversal rules such as ŌĆ£if actor = novice and task = explore, prefer Pattern B after Pattern A; if regulatory risk is high, require Pattern C first.ŌĆØ That reflects what OPL does with modeling flow, what IFML does with interaction flows, and what KG-backed systems do with query-guided recommendation. ### Open questions and limitations

The field is still unresolved on four important points. First, there is **no dominant cross-domain standard** for evidence strength, confidence, and applicability scoring at the pattern entry level. Second, most strong generative examples are still **prototypes or domain-specific systems**, not mainstream UX repositories. Third, **service design** remains less formalized than ontology engineering or model-driven UI in machine-actionable formats. Fourth, even the most promising component-level standards and specs still operate below the level of full pattern-language semantics. Those limits do not block action, but they do mean that any serious modern pattern language will need to define some of its own conventions rather than waiting for a settled industry standard to appear.
