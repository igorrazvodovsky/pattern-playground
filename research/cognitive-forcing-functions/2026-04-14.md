## Context

*Topic*: cognitive forcing functions in AI-assisted decision-making.

*Interaction*: A user faces a discrete accept/reject decision on an AI recommendation. The interface interposes a moment of active cognition (wait, decide-first, articulate, complete) before acceptance, so the user engages judgement instead of rubber-stamping the system's answer.

*Setting*: high-stakes AI-assisted decision-making — medical, legal, financial, safety-critical, or analytical work. Assumes the user has domain competence. Not warranted when stakes are low, user lacks evaluative competence, or speed dominates. Susceptibility modulated by need-for-cognition.

*Questions driving retrieval*:
1. when does friction help vs backfire (timing-critical or low-stakes cases)
2. how do forcing-function variants compare on anchoring resistance
3. what is the evidence for wait-before-reveal specifically
4. how do individual differences (need for cognition, expertise) modulate effectiveness
5. can forcing functions train judgement over time, or only gate it in the moment

## Convergent findings

### 1. The intervention's effect is moderated by cognitive motivation, and unevenly so

*Synthesis*. Buçinca finds that CFFs benefit high-NfC users *more*, not less — the population that most needs correction (low-NfC) also has the least motivation to engage with the imposed cognition. Vasconcelos reframes non-engagement as a rational cost-benefit response rather than a cognitive bias: users strategically decide whether verification is worth its cost. Together they imply that CFF design is incomplete without companion work on the cost and benefit of the evaluation itself.

*Papers*: Buçinca et al. 2021; Vasconcelos et al. 2022.

*Disagreement*: Buçinca's frame is psychological (individual differences); Vasconcelos's is economic (rational strategy). They are complementary rather than opposed — motivation sets the threshold, cost-benefit determines whether it is crossed.

*Draft project takeaway*: Cognitive forcing interventions' effects are moderated by need-for-cognition and by the cost-benefit of verification itself; the users most in need (low-NfC, high-cost tasks) benefit least unless the intervention simultaneously lowers evaluation cost or raises its reward. Reframes CFF design as co-design with motivation structure, not a standalone friction tool.

### 2. CFF variants are mechanistically distinct, and perception diverges from effect

*Synthesis*. Ghosh compares Assumption (argument analysis) vs WhatIf (hypothesis testing): Assumption reduces overreliance without cognitive-load cost, but participants perceive WhatIf as more helpful. Ma proposes deliberative, dimension-level elicitation as a structural alternative. Xu sketches multi-agent devil's advocates. These are not flavours of one intervention — they interrupt different heuristics and surface different feedback.

*Papers*: Ghosh et al. 2026; Ma et al. 2024; Xu et al. 2026.

*Disagreement*: Ghosh measures overreliance reduction; Ma measures appropriate reliance + task performance; Xu theorises. No direct contradiction; they occupy different evaluation frames.

*Draft project takeaway*: Cognitive-forcing variants are mechanistically distinct (argument analysis, hypothesis testing, deliberative elicitation, adversarial agents) and their perceived helpfulness does not predict their effect on overreliance. Supports the story's treatment of the CFF *family* as genuinely plural; argues against any single "best" variant.

### 3. CFFs fail characteristically in continuous generation

*Synthesis*. Ashktorab finds that in LLM-assisted content generation, users produce emergent reliance behaviours — appending incorrect AI output alongside their own correct answers — that bypass the accept/reject structure the CFF assumed. Ma notes the accept/reject paradigm itself is a source of passivity, proposing deliberation as a structural alternative. Both imply that the CFF scope condition (discrete accept/reject) is load-bearing in a way the current story marks lightly.

*Papers*: Ashktorab et al. 2024; Ma et al. 2024.

*Draft project takeaway*: Cognitive forcing functions depend on discrete accept/reject structure; in continuous generation users route around the intervention by preserving AI content in their own output. Suggests the CFF family does not generalise to AI completion / generated content without reshaping the interaction paradigm, not just the friction point.

### 4. Verifiability is a precondition, not just competence

*Synthesis*. Fok & Weld argue that explanations help only when they enable verification; many decision tasks fundamentally do not support verification regardless of explanation design. The story's current preconditions (high stakes, domain competence, discrete decision) do not name verifiability.

*Papers*: Fok & Weld 2023; Vasconcelos et al. 2022 (via verification cost).

*Draft project takeaway*: Task verifiability is a third precondition for cognitive forcing functions alongside high stakes and user competence: forcing thought only helps when thought can land on a checkable answer. Many decision tasks resist this regardless of intervention.

### 5. Friction as default posture, not edge intervention (single-paper cluster)

*Synthesis*. Xu positions deliberate friction as an infrastructural requirement rather than a UX flourish — as zero-friction becomes the default, absence of CFFs becomes the design choice. Position paper; empirical grounding comes from a meta-analysis of HCI publication trends rather than user studies.

*Papers*: Xu et al. 2026.

*Draft project takeaway*: As fluent AI interfaces become the default, friction becomes an infrastructural stance rather than an edge intervention. Relevant to how the project frames *appropriate reliance* as a baseline commitment of interaction design, not a corrective applied case-by-case.

### 6. Strategy-graded feedback may compound into judgement (single-paper cluster)

*Synthesis*. Schemmer et al. argue that human learning mediates appropriate reliance — static mental models of the AI are insufficient. This implies CFF variants that expose the user's own judgement before or alongside the AI's ("human goes first", rationale logging) have a distinct mechanism: they produce strategy-graded feedback that may compound over repeated interactions.

*Papers*: Schemmer et al. 2023.

*Draft project takeaway*: Some cognitive forcing variants — human goes first, rationale logging — produce strategy-graded feedback that plausibly trains calibration over repeated interaction, rather than only gating judgement in the moment. Distinguishes them from in-moment variants (wait, on-demand reveal) and points toward evaluation over sessions rather than single trials.
